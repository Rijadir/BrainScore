Understanding IQ

A comprehensive guide to intelligence quotient

What is IQ?

IQ (Intelligence Quotient) is a score derived from standardized tests designed to assess human intelligence. The average IQ is set at 100, with a standard deviation of 15 points. This means about 68% of the population scores between 85 and 115.

How is it Measured?

IQ tests measure various cognitive abilities through specialized subtests. These combine to form a Full Scale IQ (FSIQ) that represents overall cognitive ability. The main index scores include:

  • WMI (Working Memory Index) — Measures the ability to hold and manipulate information in short-term memory while performing mental operations.
  • FRI (Fluid Reasoning Index) — Assesses the capacity to think logically, solve novel problems, and identify patterns without relying on prior knowledge.
  • PSI (Processing Speed Index) — Evaluates the speed and accuracy of visual scanning, decision-making, and mental processing of simple information.
  • VSI (Visual Spatial Index) — Measures the ability to analyze and synthesize visual-spatial information, including mental rotation and spatial reasoning.
  • VCI (Verbal Comprehension Index) — Assesses verbal reasoning, vocabulary, and the ability to express knowledge verbally.

The Bell Curve

IQ scores follow a normal distribution (bell curve). Most people (about 95%) score between 70 and 130. Scores below 70 or above 130 are considered rare, occurring in only about 2.5% of the population on each end.

What Does IQ Predict?

Research shows IQ correlates with academic performance, job performance, income, and health outcomes. However, IQ is just one measure of cognitive ability and doesn't capture creativity, emotional intelligence, or practical skills.

Nature vs Nurture

IQ is influenced by both genetic and environmental factors. Studies suggest heritability ranges from 50-80% in adults. Education, nutrition, and cognitive stimulation during childhood also play significant roles.

Can IQ Change?

While IQ is relatively stable in adulthood, it can change during childhood and adolescence. Factors like education, cognitive training, and lifestyle can influence IQ scores. The Flynn Effect shows average IQ has increased over generations.
